2. French Pronunciation Decoded
French spelling looks intimidating, but it follows consistent rules. Once you learn these patterns, you can pronounce almost any French word correctly – even words you’ve never seen before.

2.1 The Golden Rules

THE 3 MOST IMPORTANT PRONUNCIATION RULES
1. SILENT FINAL CONSONANTS: Most consonants at the end of a word are silent. "Petit" = puh-TEE (the t is silent). "Vous parlez" = voo par-LAY (the z is silent). Exception: C, R, F, L are usually pronounced (remember: "CaReFuL").
2. STRESS IS ALWAYS ON THE LAST SYLLABLE: Unlike English, French stress is predictable. Every word and phrase is stressed on the final syllable. "chocolat" = sho-ko-LA. "information" = an-for-ma-see-ON.
3. NASAL VOWELS: When a vowel is followed by N or M (and no vowel after), it becomes nasal – the air goes through your nose. "bon" = boh(n), "pain" = pa(n), "vin" = va(n). The N/M itself is barely pronounced.

2.5 The CaReFuL Rule (Final Consonants That ARE Pronounced)
Most final consonants are silent, but C, R, F, L are usually pronounced:
	•	C: avec (a-VEK), sac (SAK), parc (PARK)
	•	R: pour (POOR), sur (SÜR), bonjour (bon-ZHOOR)
	•	F: chef (SHEF), sportif (spor-TEEF), neuf (NUHF)
	•	L: animal (a-nee-MAL), hotel (oh-TEL), avril (a-VREEL)
Exceptions exist (estomac = silent C, monsieur = silent R), but CaReFuL works 90% of the time.

4.2 The Spoken French Shortcut: 3 Forms Sound Identical
This is the single biggest shortcut in spoken French:

CONJUGATION SHORTCUT: JE, TU, IL/ELLE ALL SOUND THE SAME
For ALL regular -er verbs (which are 80%+ of French verbs), the je, tu, and il/elle forms are pronounced IDENTICALLY:
je parle, tu parles, il/elle parle = all pronounced "PARL"
je mange, tu manges, il/elle mange = all pronounced "MAHNZH"
je travaille, tu travailles, il/elle travaille = all pronounced "tra-VIE"
This means for spoken French, you only need to learn 4 sounds per verb: je/tu/il (same), nous, vous, ils/elles.
For NCLC 5 (speaking test only), this cuts your conjugation workload nearly in half.

4.4 Future Tense Cheat: Use "aller + infinitive"
Instead of learning the formal future tense, just use "aller" (to go) + the infinitive. This works exactly like English "I’m going to...":
	•	Je vais manger = I’m going to eat
	•	Tu vas travailler = You’re going to work
	•	On va partir = We’re going to leave
	•	Ils vont venir = They’re going to come
This construction is used constantly in everyday French and is 100% acceptable at NCLC 5.

4.5 Past Tense Cheat: Passé Composé Formula
The most common past tense in spoken French follows a simple formula:
Subject + avoir (conjugated) + past participle

	•	J’ai mangé = I ate (I have eaten)
	•	Tu as parlé = You spoke
	•	Il a travaillé = He worked
	•	Nous avons fini = We finished
	•	Vous avez pris = You took

Past participle shortcuts: -er verbs → -é (manger→mangé), -ir verbs → -i (finir→fini), -re verbs → -u (vendre→vendu). This covers 80% of past tense situations.

THE "DR. & MRS. VANDERTRAMP" VERBS USE ÊTRE (not avoir)
About 15 verbs use être instead of avoir in the past tense. These are mostly verbs of MOVEMENT:
Devenir, Revenir, Monter, Retourner, Sortir, Venir, Aller, Naître, Descendre, Entrer, Rentrer, Tomber, Rester, Arriver, Mourir, Partir
With être, the past participle agrees with the subject: Elle est alléE (she went), Ils sont arrivÉS (they arrived).
Mnemonic: these are all about coming, going, arriving, leaving, being born, or dying.

5. Essential Grammar Patterns

5.1 Negation: Sandwich the Verb
To make any sentence negative, put NE...PAS around the conjugated verb:
	•	Je parle → Je NE parle PAS (I don’t speak)
	•	Il mange → Il NE mange PAS (He doesn’t eat)
	•	Nous avons fini → Nous N’avons PAS fini (We haven’t finished)

SPOKEN FRENCH SHORTCUT
In everyday spoken French, the "ne" is almost always DROPPED:
Je parle pas. Il mange pas. C’est pas vrai.
You’ll hear this constantly on TV5MONDE. It’s not "wrong" – it’s how real French people talk.
For the TEF exam, use both ne...pas to be safe, but understand that native speakers often skip the ne.

5.2 Asking Questions: 3 Ways (Easiest First)
From most casual to most formal:
	•	1. Rising intonation (easiest): Tu parles français? = You speak French? (Just raise your voice at the end, exactly like English.)
	•	2. Est-ce que (medium): Est-ce que tu parles français? = Do you speak French? (Put "est-ce que" before any statement to make it a question.)
	•	3. Inversion (formal): Parles-tu français? = Do you speak French? (Flip the verb and subject. Used in writing and formal speech.)
For NCLC 5, methods 1 and 2 are all you need. Method 1 is what French people use 90% of the time.

70 = soixante-dix (literally: 60 + 10)

8. Liaison & Connected Speech
Liaison is when a normally silent final consonant is pronounced because the next word starts with a vowel. This is what makes French sound "flowing" rather than choppy.

8.1 Mandatory Liaisons (Must Do)
	•	Article + noun: les_amis (lay-ZA-mee), un_homme (uh-NOM), des_étudiants (day-ZAY-tu-dee-ah(n))
	•	Subject pronoun + verb: nous_avons (noo-ZA-voh(n)), vous_êtes (voo-ZET), ils_ont (eel-ZOH(N))
	•	Adjective + noun: petit_enfant (puh-TEE-tah(n)-fah(n)), bon_ami (boh-NA-mee)
	•	After prepositions: en_Italie (ah(n)-nee-ta-LEE), dans_un (dah(n)-ZU(N)), chez_elle (shay-ZEL)

8.2 Liaison Sound Changes
	•	s/x → Z sound: les amis = lay-ZA-mee, deux heures = duh-ZUHR
	•	d → T sound: grand homme = grah(n)-TOM
	•	n → N sound: un ami = uh-NA-mee, bon appetit = boh-NA-pay-tee
	•	t → T sound: petit enfant = puh-TEE-tah(n)-fah(n)

8.3 Elision (Dropping Vowels)
When a short word ending in -e meets a word starting with a vowel, the -e drops and is replaced by an apostrophe:
	•	le + ami = l’ami (not "le ami")
	•	je + ai = j’ai (not "je ai")
	•	ne + ai = n’ai (not "ne ai")
	•	de + eau = d’eau
	•	que + il = qu’il
This is mandatory, not optional. You’ll never hear a French person say "je ai" – it’s always "j’ai."

	•	Je suis pour / Je suis contre = I am for / I am against
	•	En conclusion... = In conclusion...

10.3 Recovery Strategies (When You’re Stuck)
	•	Forgot a word? Describe it: "C’est une chose qui..." (It’s a thing that...) or "C’est comme..." (It’s like...)
	•	Made a mistake? Self-correct naturally: "Non, pardon, je veux dire..." (No, sorry, I mean...)
	•	Don’t understand the question? "Excusez-moi, pouvez-vous reformuler la question?" (Can you rephrase the question?)
	•	Running out of things to say? "Et aussi..." (And also...) then add any related detail.
	•	Completely lost? Switch to a topic you know: "C’est intéressant, mais je voudrais aussi parler de..." (That’s interesting, but I’d also like to talk about...)

THE #1 TEF SPEAKING SECRET
The examiner isn’t grading your grammar perfection. They’re grading your ability to COMMUNICATE.
A confident speaker who makes grammar mistakes but keeps talking will ALWAYS score higher than a hesitant speaker who produces perfect sentences but with long silences.
Keep. Talking. Use fillers. Describe around words you don’t know. Self-correct and move on. NEVER go silent.

NOTE
This tutorial covers Standard French. Québécois French (used in most of Canada outside Quebec City and Montreal) has differences in pronunciation, vocabulary, and expressions. Exposure to Canadian French via Francolab and TV5 Québec is recommended alongside this guide.
